## Core Read

The Third Reflection is a night-glass predator of drawdown flood-reed basins beneath a high escarpment route. It is most often seen in reflection before anyone sees the body.

The locals do not say, “There’s one ahead.”

They say, “The road is looking back.”

## Apex Role

The Third Reflection controls a chain of flood-reed basins below a high escarpment route during drawdown. It does not “guard” the road in a supernatural sense. It hunts where the land tricks travelers into trusting false shallows, old reed mats, and dry-looking channels.

It is dangerous because it belongs there.

## Behavior

The protected-pressure rolls are interpreted safely: locals associate the Third Reflection with “wrong road” feelings, haunted water, and impossible reflections, but this seed does not explain any protected mystery.

The predator simply lives in places already thick with bad readings, rumor, and fear.

Observed behavior:

- Hunts mostly by forcing prey toward false shallows.
- Avoids direct pursuit unless prey is already exhausted or trapped.
- Goes still when radios crackle, machinery idles, or people argue.
- Leaves reflective mud streaks on road stones.
- Follows prey animals that know the basin better than people.
- Sometimes appears to “warn” crews only because its prey flees before reed mats fail.

What people get wrong: They think it stalks humans. Usually, humans are just clumsy animals crossing its pantry.

## Required Apex Predator Fields

### Primary Prey

Reed-crown grazers, basin haulers, skiff-birds, mud-rats, and exhausted pack animals that wander toward false shallows during drawdown.

### Competitor / Scavenger

Black-shell scavengers follow old kills and strip basin bones after the Third Reflection leaves. Their presence helps locals identify last night’s feeding line.

Competing predators avoid direct conflict but trail the basin after a kill, making the road more dangerous for hours.

### Follower / Warning Species

Tinwing skiff-birds are the warning species.

When tinwings stop landing on puddles and begin perching high on route signs, adults should stop pretending the road is fine.

Officials record this as “avian agitation.”

Children call it “counting upstairs.”

### Territory Type

A seasonal drawdown territory: one basin, three drainage throats, and a chain of old reed shelters under the escarpment road.

Its territory is not a circle. It is a route-shaped hunger.

### Route Closure Effect

The Third Reflection closes the lower escarpment road at certain hours during drawdown, especially at dawn and after heat-heavy afternoons.

It also:

- Fouls sensors and GPR through churned mineral mud, kill drag marks, and compacted reed mats.
- Makes official maps unreliable for one season.
- Forces convoy spacing changes because closely packed vehicles vibrate reed shelves.
- Creates demand for black-market guide corrections.
- Makes one route safer by keeping worse scavenger packs away.

### Settlement Adaptation

- Raised sleeping platforms.
- Down-angled lights so reflections do not confuse night watch.
- No open kitchens near basin-facing alleys after sundown.
- Children taught warning-bird behavior before official route math.
- Refuse burned or sealed before drawdown.
- Watch rotations named after reflection count: First Glass, Second Glass, Third Glass.
- Livestock kept behind sound screens when tinwings perch high.
- Market stalls close early if puddles hold “too many road markers.”

### Faction That Understands It Best

Free Scabs understand it best.

They do not control it, tame it, or worship it. They read it. They use its territory as unofficial border logic and hidden-route protection.

“Third Glass has the basin. Pay me tomorrow if tomorrow is still interested.”

### Faction That Misuses It

World Works and some contract authorities misuse it.

They blame the predator for delays, missing cargo, and sensor failures that were often caused by bad maintenance, fraudulent route reports, or human sabotage. This lets them convert ecological risk into paperwork fog.

Prime Ops may classify it as a nuisance hazard, which locals consider a dangerous understatement.

### Local Warning Sign

Three reflections where there should be one.

A puddle shows the road marker, the sky, and then a third low shape crossing between them. Children are trained to look away from the animal and count the reflections instead.

Local rule: If the third reflection moves before the reeds move, leave.

### What Officials Get Wrong

Officials think the children are repeating superstition.

The children are doing field geometry.

Children know the third reflection means one of three practical things:

- The predator is low and close.
- The water is deeper than it looks.
- The reed mat has hollowed underneath.

Any one of those is enough to turn the convoy around.

### What Happens If It Disappears

The route seems safer for three weeks.

Then the reed-crown grazers overfeed the basin margins, black-shell scavengers multiply, lesser predators spread toward camp edges, and crews start crossing false flats because nothing scares the warning birds upward.

The Third Reflection is not good. It is not kind.

But its absence removes a boundary the map had quietly been using.

### Encounter Style

Avoidance, silence, reflection-counting, route correction.

Best encounter types:

- Distant reflection with no visible body.
- Tinwing skiff-birds abandoning puddles.
- A Free Scab guide forcing a convoy to shut off lights.
- Children stopping adults from crossing.
- Predator interrupts a human ambush by making everyone change plans.
- Body never fully shown, only its effect on route behavior.
- The predator saves someone indirectly by forcing them off a doomed road.

## Story Hooks

### 1. The Convoy That Turned Around

A convoy refuses a Free Scab guide’s warning until a child points to a puddle. The road marker has three reflections. The adult crew sees nothing in the reeds.

### 2. The False Closure

A contract house claims the Third Reflection caused a missing cargo delay. The locals know the predator never crossed that drainage throat. Someone used its name as a paper-blade.

### 3. The Useful Monster

A crew thinks the predator is hunting them. Actually, it is shadowing fleeing prey that knows the basin is about to slump. If the crew reads the movement correctly, they survive. If they shoot, shout, or accelerate, they become part of the wrong story.

### 4. The Empty Basin Problem

The Third Reflection disappears from a route section. Everyone celebrates until lesser scavenger-predators arrive, warning birds stop behaving predictably, and the “safe” crossing becomes a ledger of broken axles.

## First Scene Seed

The convoy reaches the lower escarpment road at dawn.

The official map says the crossing is green. The GPR says the reed mat is solid. The driver wants to keep moving because rain is forming a bruise on the horizon.

Then a little girl from the reed-camp drops her breakfast tin into a puddle, not by accident. The tin rocks once. Twice. The road marker appears in the water. The sky appears behind it.

Then a third reflection slides between them.

No body. No roar. No attack.

Just a wet black jawline moving through a place where nothing visible moves.

The Free Scab guide raises one hand and every adult who knows the basin lowers their voice at the same time.
