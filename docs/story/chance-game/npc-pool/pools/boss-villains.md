## Core Concept

Evek “Clutch-Saint” Marr was once a Velocisapien route mechanic with a ceremonial attachment to machines that had carried people through impossible corridors. He treated old engines, wounded tractors, and route rigs like witnesses. Not gods. Not pets. Witnesses.

Then came the failed rescue.

The official record says Evek violated a route ban, mishandled a machine, and caused a corridor embarrassment near a proposed extension. The unofficial story says he tried to save people after everyone cleaner had already decided they were unrecoverable.

WWISSaRD did not recruit him.

WWISSaRD preserved the problem he became.

Now Evek is kept off-books as a release asset: a grief-driven mechanic whose body has been bound to a fictional symbiotic load that reacts to old machine motion, public exposure, and panic. He is deployed when a machine wakes where it should not, when witnesses gather too quickly, or when a public spectacle needs to become a private tragedy.

Core line:

> Evek does not kill machines. He gives them last rites with his hands still inside the engine.

Short version:

> Evek is grief filed as equipment.

## Boss Arena

**Boss Arena:** Prime Ops Disciplinary Bay

A brightly lit disciplinary bay where a confiscated old machine sits behind restraint lines. Officials, clerks, mechanics, and witnesses are present because the machine woke publicly during a hearing.

The room was built for procedure.

The fight turns it into a confession booth with impact alarms.

**Arena Features:** restraint rails, evidence lights, observation glass, sealed doors, machine clamps, overhead disciplinary speakers, a public record system that keeps trying to write the wrong story, an old machine that should not be moving.

## Old Machine Guardrail

## Three-Phase Confrontation

### Phase 1: Debt Leverage

Evek speaks politely while dismantling the room’s power, evidence lights, and machine restraints.

He frames everything as debt.

The machine owes motion.

The witnesses owe silence.

The officials owe a clean report.

The player owes a choice.

**Boss Pressure:** environmental control, machine clamps, route-marker pattern attacks.

### Phase 2: Family Leverage

The symbiotic load begins reacting to people in the room: witnesses, mechanics, clerks, anyone emotionally tied to the machine or corridor incident.

Evek does not become crueler.

He becomes more precise.

**Boss Pressure:** protect bystanders, interrupt marked-target lunges, stop the arena from sorting people into liability categories.

### Phase 3: Self-Sacrifice Demand

Evek offers the cleanest ending:

Let him destroy the awakened machine, let one person sign the false confession, and everyone else walks away.

This is the villain logic: he believes one chosen lie can save the crowd.

**Boss Pressure:** Moral trap. Win by refusing his premise, not just beating his body.

## Non-Combat Weakness

**Boss Weakness:** Cannot distinguish debt from ownership.

Evek believes every saved life creates a debt, every debt becomes ownership, and every owned thing can be filed, reassigned, or erased.

He weakens when someone proves care is not a claim.

**Practical Non-Combat Options:** name him publicly as Evek Marr, not by storage category; expose the release order in front of witnesses; refuse to let the machine, a person, or Evek be treated as property; make WWISSaRD deny him publicly, breaking the release logic; use local practical knowledge to keep the old machine alive without surrendering it to Evek’s “mercy.”

## Why WWISSaRD Releases Him

WWISSaRD releases Evek because an old machine wakes publicly inside a Prime Ops disciplinary setting, creating too many witnesses, too much liability, and too many contradictory records.

Every legal option creates a bigger paper trail.

Evek is the deniable option.

He can be filed as salvage handling, emergency containment, equipment stabilization, or cleanup support.

## Why Releasing Him Makes Things Worse

Evek does not merely attack the problem.

He interprets the entire room as a machine that deserves one final, terrible repair.

His release turns one public embarrassment into:

- a witness crisis
- a Prime Ops procedural scandal
- a WWISSaRD denial event
- a machine-rights rumor
- a route-ban ghost story
- a record nobody can safely correct

The freezer solved the paperwork problem by teaching it to walk.

## Villain Morality

Evek is not evil because he enjoys harm.

He is dangerous because he believes mercy must be completed before bureaucracy arrives.

He thinks saving everyone is childish.

He thinks saving one person and burning the record is adulthood.

Core principle:

> Evek thinks he is the answer to a question everyone else was too sentimental to finish.
