## Core Concept

The Name-Silt Ledger House is a street-level Hydropolis dry-body registry annex hidden beneath a maintenance arcade where old repair rollers, drainage pumps, black-glass panels, and identity ledgers share the same chamber.

It was originally used for maintenance records, route-support logs, and low-water administrative overflow.

In sandbox story use, it now functions as a refuge and black-market registry site for people whose names, debt records, route permissions, medical classifications, or legal identities have become dangerous.

Its ledgers are never fully dry.

Pages curl. Stamps blur. Ink feathers into veinlike stains. False names grow damp at the edges first. True names do not stay clean either, which is why nobody trusts the building completely.

The place shelters people, but it also stains every version of them.

## Street-Level Description

The annex sits below a maintenance arcade in a low-water dry-body stretch of Hydropolis. The public-facing level looks like an abandoned service mouth: roll-up shutters, corroded registry signage, dry pump housings, and a dead worklight that still clicks once every few minutes.

Inside, the repair bay and registry office were never properly separated. Old repair rollers sit beside ledger racks. Drainage pumps breathe stale coolant smell into shelves of bad paperwork. Black-glass panels lean against the walls like dark mirrors that learned clerical work.

This is not a clean archive. It is a pressure chamber where civic process, street survival, black-market document work, and community protection all crowd around the same damp table.

## Name-Silt Rule

**Name-silt** is the local nickname for gray-black residue that gathers beneath shelves, ledgers, drawers, and record cabinets inside the Ledger House.

It may be made of:

- Coolant residue.
- Paper dust.
- Mineral grit.
- Black-glass powder.
- Old repair debris.
- Damp registry mold.
- Other untested maintenance residue.

**Canon-Safe Rule:** Name-silt is environmental weirdness with legal and emotional bite, not confirmed metaphysics.

Do not canonize name-silt as a new planet-wide substance. Do not turn it into a drug, magical mineral, Contact Frame byproduct, or universal identity detector.

## The Damp Test

The Damp Test is a local black-market practice. It is believed, feared, faked, misread, exploited, and sometimes coincidentally useful.

Black-market mapmakers or identity fixers write a name on scrap paper and place it on the pressure-roller plate.

Local interpretations:

- If the paper stays dry, the name is "usable."
- If it beads with moisture, the name is "claimed."
- If the ink runs completely, the name is "hungry."

**Guardrail:** The Damp Test is not a reliable universal identity test.

## The West Doorway

The west doorway opens into a narrow alcove full of:

- Black-glass maintenance panels.
- Old coolant valves.
- Hanging strips of ledger paper.
- Damp record strings.
- Sound-carrying wall gaps.

**Local Taboo:** Never speak a birth name inside the doorway.

**Practical Explanation:** The alcove carries sound into adjoining rooms.

**Fearful Explanation:** The building remembers names spoken there.

**Useful Explanation:** Both stories keep desperate people quiet.

## How It Complicates

Every document here leaves residue.

The protagonists may get the papers they need, but the name-silt gives away which drawer they opened.

Complications to preserve:

- A forged name begins soaking through its folder during negotiation.
- The pressure rollers activate to "dry" a ledger, threatening to destroy it.
- A black-market mapmaker notices that the protagonists' route name is damp.
- Sella refuses to give someone a new identity because the available file is "still grieving."
- Contract court agents arrive with waterproof evidence bags, which means they have been here before.

## Object / Clue / Landmark

A gate token hangs near the west doorway on a hook meant for coolant keys. The token should not be used to confirm gateway mechanics, Craton law, Contact Frame rules, or cosmology.

Sandbox-safe uses:

- It proves someone once had permission they now deny.
- It identifies a missing repair crew.
- It was traded as collateral for a false name.
- It opens nothing, but everyone acts like it might.
- It belongs to someone who should not have been near this Hydropolis dry-body annex.

Best version: the token is less important for what it opens and more important for who signed it out.

## Main Hazard

Old pressure rollers remain inside the maintenance chamber. Some are inactive. Some are mis-tagged. Some can start when the pumps wake, when someone hits an old repair switch, or when a clerk tries to "dry" a ledger too quickly.

Hazard uses:

- A character can be pinned against a maintenance track.
- A ledger shelf can be dragged toward the machinery.
- A gate token can fall under the rollers.
- Someone can threaten to destroy evidence physically instead of legally.
- The machinery can wake during a negotiation, forcing everyone to move before they can lie again.

## How to Introduce It

The characters arrive through a low-water maintenance arcade that should be empty. Instead, a paper tag hangs from a pump lever, still damp around the handwriting.

The public registry door is sealed. The repair hatch beside it is open.

Inside, black-market route maps dry from wires. Ledger drawers are built into coolant lockers. A clerk's desk has been bolted between two machine rails.

A courier or child says:

"Don't use your clean name here. Clean names get billed."

Then Sella Vane-of-No-Doors asks who sent them, who owns their names, and whether they need shelter or correction.
